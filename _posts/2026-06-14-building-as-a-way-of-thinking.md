We have heard the proverb: "Think before you act."

It is valuable advice. In an exam, we are expected to read the question properly, think through the answer, and then start writing. Similarly in software development, we are often asked to think through the problem, ask clarifying questions, plan the solution and only then start implementation.

The same applies to many decisions in life. Our lives are usually better when we give everything enough thought and not act hastily. This advice has probably saved us from many blunders.

But what happens when the problem is too complex to think through entirely in our mind?

Our minds have limited processing capacity. We may feel that we can think through the whole problem in our mind, but in reality, we often miss important nuances.

The obvious solution is to write things down or draw diagrams. This helps us see relationships, gaps, and assumptions that are hard to notice when everything is only within our mind. Writing and drawing are not separate from thinking. They are extensions of it. We write and draw diagrams to think better.

But what if the system is so complex and nuanced that even diagrams become difficult to follow?

Personally, when I face such complex problems, I like to build a model or a simulation to see the system working. Not a polished end product or something production-ready. Just something good enough to give me clarity.

I know this may sound counterintuitive to the proverb "think before you act", but it is not. I don't build without thinking. I build to think better.

And as I build, my clarity improves. I start to see how pieces that earlier seemed unrelated now fit together. I discover what works and what does not. I gain a level of clarity that no amount of writing or diagrams could have given me.

This is not acting without thinking. It is thinking through action.

And this is not a foreign concept. Software developers sometimes create prototypes to test whether an idea is viable. Designers create wireframes before building the final website. Scientists run experiments to understand reality better. Writers start with a rough draft, because writing that draft helps them think.

A plan made entirely in the mind can feel complete, but the moment we start building, hidden assumptions become visible. We realize that a requirement is unclear, an interaction is more complicated than expected, or a solution that looked elegant in theory is impractical.

But not every thought needs to become a prototype. That would slow life down unnecessarily. Simpler problems, like deciding what to eat or what to wear, can be thought through entirely in our minds. When problems are more complex, we may write things down or draw diagrams to improve clarity.

But some problems reach a point where thinking, writing, and drawing diagrams are still not enough.

Building, when done thoughtfully, is not premature execution. It is exploration. It is a way of making thought visible, testable, and improvable.

Maybe the point is not to replace thinking with building. Maybe it is to notice when thought has become too complex to be contained within our minds.

Because sometimes we do not build because we already understand. We build because we are trying to.
