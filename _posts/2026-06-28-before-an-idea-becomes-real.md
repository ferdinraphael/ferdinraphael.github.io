We all have ideas. Some completely random, some just passing thoughts, and then some we keep returning to. But most of these ideas remain ideas and never materialize.

The obvious reason is ideas are cheap but execution is expensive. Most of us don't have the time, skills, money, or energy to turn an idea into reality. And carrying an idea through multiple potential failures can feel daunting.

But one of the biggest barriers to executing the idea is not starting. Looking at the entire idea may feel scary.

Can I really execute all this?

What will I have to sacrifice for this?

Can I really spare time, money and energy for this?

What if the idea is flawed and I fail?

That last question is especially powerful. After spending a lot of time, money and energy on the idea, if it fails it will hurt a lot. And this fear can cause paralysis.

We might think that we need to come up with the perfect plan to reduce our losses. But the more we plan, the more daunting the idea becomes. And eventually we might just get overwhelmed and give up on it.

What if there was a better way? What if we didn't need the perfect plan?

An idea is actually the destination we want to reach. Even though the path is unclear, we can take just one small step that is within our ability. Something that takes only a reasonable amount of time, money and energy.

This one step would play two roles. Firstly, it would break the paralysis and let us come one step closer to our idea. Secondly, it would let us collect real data, test assumptions, and help refine our idea better.

A positive response can show that our idea is viable and can give us the confidence to push through and execute the idea one step at a time.

A negative response can show what is lacking in our idea, or which assumptions turned out to be false. This can ground the idea in a practical setting and allow us to improve the idea and tackle those issues. No one can optimize or improve an idea that exists only in the mind.

Some ideas may turn out to be highly unrealistic and fundamentally flawed. Even this would turn out to be a teaching moment. And failing early also means the time, money and energy put into this is small compared to the cost of chasing the entire idea.

And in most cases, executing that first step also causes internal growth. We can suddenly think more clearly. We can envision the second and maybe even the third step clearly. Each step takes us closer to turning the idea into a reality.

Not every idea needs to be chased. But the ones we keep returning to deserve the first step.
