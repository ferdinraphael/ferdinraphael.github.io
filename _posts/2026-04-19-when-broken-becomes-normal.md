There is a pattern I have seen more often than I would like.

A process fails. Someone creates a quick workaround. The workaround works well enough and everyone adjusts around it. New employees are taught it. Months later, nobody remembers that it was temporary. It has now become the routine. The system is still functional, but only because people have absorbed the burden of what the system failed to solve.

At first, the team may be applauded for keeping things running. But the people who continue putting in the invisible effort to sustain the workaround are hardly noticed. They silently bear the cost of the broken system.

There is a dangerous difference between solving a crisis and adapting to a chronic condition. When a workaround lasts long enough, it becomes part of how things are done. People often begin to resist replacing the broken system because the disruption of change feels riskier than the burden they have learned to carry. The tool is no longer serving the mission. The mission is slowly warped to fit the tool.

Workarounds are not inherently problematic. In fact, they are often necessary. They allow systems to function in the face of unexpected failure and buy time to recover properly. The issue begins when temporary survival mechanisms quietly become permanent design. What was meant to bridge a gap eventually defines the path itself.

You can see this pattern almost everywhere once you start looking for it.

In software teams, it appears when the same data has to be manually corrected every day because fixing the root cause never becomes a priority. The process works, but only because someone quietly compensates for its failures.

In organizations, it appears when recurring problems trigger the same emergency response every time. The organization becomes proficient at managing symptoms while the underlying cause remains untouched.

The pattern extends beyond the workplace. Unfair expectations are excused as "just how things work." People reorganize their lives around the unfairness until questioning it begins to look unreasonable. What began as a compromise becomes accepted as the natural order.

Broken systems do not just create inefficiency. They consume attention, time, and emotional energy. Over time, they erode trust, reduce initiative, and quietly discourage people from questioning how things work. They also shrink something less visible but more important: the capacity to imagine alternatives.

Maybe the first step is not to fix everything immediately. Maybe it is simply to notice what has become invisible: the repeated workaround, the person quietly carrying the gap, the cost everyone has stopped counting.

Because sometimes a system is not working well. People have simply become very good at making it look like it is.
