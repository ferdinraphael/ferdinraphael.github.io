For most practical purposes, I am a software developer. That is the easiest way to introduce myself.

And it is quite true. I have spent 15 years in this field. Development has trained me to break problems down, trace dependencies, debug hidden causes, and build structures.

Every developer spends a fair amount of time working with bugs. Some are easy to fix. Some are messy. Some look small on the surface, but turn out to be symptoms of a much deeper problem. Debugging these issues, identifying root causes, and providing fixes is part of a developer's routine.

But over time, I have started noticing that software is not the only thing with bugs.
Teams have bugs. Work cultures have bugs. Education has bugs. Families inherit bugs. Societies normalize bugs and call them "just the way things are."

Some of these bugs are also manifestations of much deeper problems in our society. And I wonder if someone is truly fixing them, or just patching them up.

Earlier, when I used to think of the future, I thought about technology, careers, systems, and the brokenness of a world that often felt beyond my hands.

Then I became a father, and the future stopped being abstract. It became someone I could hold.

That changed the way I look at the world. It made every question feel very real and close to home. What kind of systems are we building? What kind of work are we normalizing? What kind of education are we accepting? What kind of future are we preparing for people who did not choose the world they are inheriting?

Somewhere along the way, I started feeling the same discomfort with how we look at people.

Everyone is more than a label.

A developer is not just a developer. A teacher is not just a teacher. A parent is not just a parent. A quiet person is not empty. A struggling person is not only their struggle. A successful person is not only their success.

There is always more history, more contradiction, more longing, more fear, more goodness, more unfinished becoming than we can see from outside.

Maybe this is why I have become uncomfortable with advice that treats people like categories.

"Developers should..."

"Parents should..."

"Young people should..."

"Leaders should..."

There might be truth in those sentences, but there is also something missing.

Something human disappears when the person becomes only the category.

So yes, I am a developer.

But that is not the whole story.

And maybe that is one quiet hope I carry into this space: to leave a fuller trail of myself, and to learn to look for the fuller trail in others.
