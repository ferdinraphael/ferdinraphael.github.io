We encounter broken systems everywhere. Some cause minor inconveniences that we simply learn to live with. Others become frustrating enough that we create workarounds and accept them as "just the way things are."

Then there are systems so frustrating that even maintaining those workarounds becomes exhausting. In such cases, the easiest conclusion is often that the system is outdated and needs to be rebuilt.

Starting over may give emotional relief by letting us envision a future where the system is built right this time. And while we are at it, we can replace the old technology, clean up the design, and finally do things properly. No more workarounds. No more frustration. No more outdated systems. Definitely feels like a win.

This is true not only of software, but also of teams, organizational policies, societies, and even our own patterns and habits — things that may once have solved a problem but now create new ones.
But a broken system is rarely made only of mistakes. It may also contain years of adaptation. There may be undocumented business knowledge that has long been forgotten. It could also contain safeguards or exceptions created for edge cases that nobody remembers. What looks like disorder may sometimes be history that was never properly recorded.

But hidden history cannot always excuse indefinite frustration. Some systems really are unsafe or too fragile to continue. Sometimes rebuilding is the responsible choice.

Even then, rebuilding should not replace understanding. Otherwise, the new system might recreate old failures using cleaner technology.

When people resist a rebuild, it's easy to conclude that they are just afraid of change. But their resistance may come from their awareness of nuances that the new system might not yet have considered. To them, "we are going to rebuild it properly" may sound less like a promise of improvement and more like a dismissal of everything they have learned, adapted, and improvised over the years.

Maybe rebuilding and tolerating are not our only choices.

Sometimes the first step is to understand and document the system. That process may reveal that the whole system is not equally broken. One area may be causing most of the trouble. And fixing that one part may be the least costly path, both technically and emotionally.

Even if documenting it reveals the need for a full rebuild, it might not need to be done all at once. It could be migrated area by area, reducing the risk of losing something important in the process.
The difficult part is not admitting something is broken. People often know that already. The difficult part is understanding what kind of brokenness we are dealing with, and what kind of response it deserves.

Not everything broken needs to be rebuilt.

But everything we choose to rebuild deserves to be understood first.
